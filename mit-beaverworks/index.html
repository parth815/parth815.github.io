INTRODUCTION

This was a four-week long project, with the final challenge incorporating elements from all the previous weeks. The goal is to enable a 1/10 scale racecar to move as quickly and with as few collision as possible when navigating in a foreign environment containing unknown obstacles. The course involves several sharp turns in addition to the ability to follow walls and avoid obstacles that can be places anywhere within the environment. This is a timed race against eight other teams, with the winners being the team with the fastest car.

The ultimate goal of this challenge is to reach the end of the course in less time than it takes any other team's car to do so. The teams will have the opportunity to take a possible shortcut based on the car's color detection, as detecting a green obstacle will allow it to take the shortcut by turning right, but detecting a red one will mean it will turn left and go a longer way. Crashing into obstacles does not reduce the score of the team, but it indirectly influences the score by requiring the robot to take extra time to back out and accelerate again.

The challenge at the end of the first week involved our car being placed between two walls and having to follow a wall of its choice. Week 2 Challenge

The challenge at the end of the second week involved our car having to decide whether to turn left or right at a T-intersection, based on what color the piece of paper ahead of it is. Afterwards, it had to follow a wall, but unlike last week's challenge, the wall was curved. Our car made the right decision at the intersection and turned properly, but did not follow the wall all the way through.

Week 3 Challenge

The challenge at the end of the third week involved our car having to move through a course while avoiding crashing into walls and randomly placed obstacles; in addition, it had to detect the papers on the blobs, take a picture of them, and identify the color of the paper. In the actual challenge, our code ran poorly and our car crashed multiple times. However, on the Monday following that, the team implemented a potential fields algorithm that worked much more smoothly and had almost no collisions. This video is a demonstration of that code in effect.

Technical Challenge 1

In the first technical challenge, the car had to again explore space as it did in the week 3 challenge. However, the walls were much further about and the course was in the shape of an hourglass, with a narrow neck connecting two elliptical courses. Thus, the car had to make many important turning decisions on it own, and it was never able to make it through the hourglass opening. However, it did not crash into an obstacle once (our car was supposedly the only one that did so) and was able to capture photos of all the blobs in the vicinity. Overall, our team succeeded in this first challenge.

Final Challenge Dry Run

In the first dry run for the final challenge, our car clearly did not perform very well. It oscillated wildly, but predictably, between the two walls throughout the course, which drastically reduced our time. While it did recognize that it could not take the shortcut route earlier in the course, this would not matter much if it was this slow. After this run, we implemented a derivative error into our potential fields algorithm, with the result being shown in the next video.

Final Challenge Dry Run (Unofficial)

In this unofficial dry run that our team made anyway, just as a final test for the racecar, our car performed much more efficiently. It was nearly in the center of the path for the entire turn, before making a sharp turn after exiting it. After each oscillation it makes, it clearly turns less and less with each, meaning the derivative function was working and the overall error was gradually diminishing. This was the code we used in the final challenge, and it served us to reach fifth place.
